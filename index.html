Norden builds a small number of durable tools for public-sector location data. We work on one problem in particular: giving local governments a stable, maintained way to refer to the same real‑world place across the many systems that describe it.

A single house can appear as a situs address in assessment, a service location in a utility system, a point in a GIS layer, an address on an open permit, and an access point in a public-safety dataset — five records, five identifiers, none aware of the others. Keeping them aligned is work that every location project quietly repeats. We think that work deserves a durable home rather than a fresh start each time.

How we build

We prefer software that does one thing well, keeps its promises across years of change, and gets out of the way. Open formats, inspectable data, human-reviewable decisions, and no dependencies we don't need. The registry should be easy to scope, approve, audit, and — if it ever comes to it — walk away from.

What we're working on

Masora

A location registry for local government. Masora maintains one durable record for each real-world place, links the source records that refer to it, keeps evidence and review history, and treats conflicts between sources as records worth keeping rather than noise to suppress. It is a maintained identity layer for places — not a GIS platform, geocoder, or one-time cleanup.

Arlington Commercial Vitality Atlas

An open-data civic map for understanding where Arlington's commercial corridors are strong, stressed, and adapting. The v0.1 prototype covers the Rosslyn–Ballston corridor and makes the commercial-tax-base challenge spatially legible — down to which buildings, parcels, and reuse opportunities matter most.

Open data, in the open

Much of the location data that shapes a community is already public — parcels, assessments, zoning, permits, transit. We build in the open where we can, publish reproducible pipelines from public datasets, and treat civic data as a shared asset rather than a private moat. The Atlas is our first public exploration in that vein, and it leans on the same place-identity idea that Masora is built around.

If reconciling address, parcel, utility, public-safety, or asset data is a recurring problem for your jurisdiction or your firm, we'd welcome a conversation.
