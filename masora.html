White paper

Masora

A location registry for local government.

Executive summary

Local governments already have address and location data. The challenge is that the same real-world place is often represented differently across 9‑1‑1, assessment, permitting, utilities, public works, GIS, and other systems. As those systems evolve independently, keeping records aligned becomes a recurring reconciliation problem.

Public-sector engineering, geospatial, and infrastructure firms routinely solve this problem by reconciling sources and creating authoritative datasets. But those deliverables begin aging as soon as the underlying systems change.

Masora is designed to provide the missing layer: stable place identity. It maintains one durable record for each real-world place, links the source records that refer to it, preserves evidence and review history, tracks conflicts, and publishes a registry that other systems can reference.

What Masora is not

Masora is not a GIS platform, geocoder, address-validation tool, or one-time cleanup project. It is a maintained identity layer for places.

For firms that serve local government, Masora turns recurring reconciliation work into a maintained client asset rather than a static project artifact. Masora is ready for an initial local-government deployment and for partner-supported delivery with firms that already serve public-sector GIS, infrastructure, utility, asset, emergency-management, NG9‑1‑1 readiness, and data programs.

About the name

Masora is pronounced mah-SOR-ah. The name is drawn from the Masoretic tradition, a body of scholarship concerned with preserving and faithfully transmitting an authoritative text across centuries of copying. The Masoretes did more than guard the main text: they recorded variant readings and unusual forms in the margins, preserving disagreement rather than erasing it.

Both ideas fit the product's purpose. Masora helps local governments maintain trusted place identity across changing source systems, records, addresses, and geometries — and, like those marginal notes, it treats conflicts between sources as records worth keeping rather than noise to suppress.

The problem: the same place is represented across many systems

Most local-government location-data projects begin with the same hidden task: determining which records refer to the same real-world place.

A single real-world place might appear as an address point in GIS, a situs address in assessment, a service location in a utility system, a permit address in permitting, a structure or access point in a public-safety dataset, and a feature in a public-works or asset system.

Each record may be correct for its own operational purpose. But each may also use a different identifier, address format, geometry, status, source authority, and update cycle. Even when the individual systems are functioning properly, the jurisdiction still needs a reliable way to know whether those records refer to the same place.

This is why reconciliation keeps returning. The problem is not simply bad data. It is that local-government systems describe overlapping pieces of the same physical world without sharing a durable place identity.

Why existing identifiers are not enough

Most source systems already contain identifiers, but those identifiers are usually scoped to the system that created them. A parcel ID identifies a parcel record. A utility account identifies a billing relationship. A permit number identifies a permitting record. A GIS object ID identifies a feature. A road centerline segment identifies part of a street network. An address point identifies a location used by an address-management or public-safety workflow.

Those identifiers matter, and Masora does not replace them. The problem is that none of them, by itself, provides a maintained cross-system identity for the real-world place those records may describe.

That gap becomes visible whenever a jurisdiction needs to answer practical questions across systems: which permits belong to this place, which utility service records refer to it, which address point represents it, which parcel contains it, which road segment provides access, which source is current, and which records disagree. Without a shared place identity, those questions are answered repeatedly through project-specific matching, manual review, spreadsheets, scripts, or one-time GIS deliverables.

Why static reconciliation fails

The challenge is not creating a reconciled dataset. The challenge is keeping it authoritative.

The inputs are not static. Addresses are corrected or renamed. Parcels split or merge. Road centerlines are edited. Site and structure address points are added, moved, or retired. Utility service records change. Permits are opened and closed. Assessment records are updated. Boundaries and service areas are revised.

Each change may be valid within its source system, but each change can weaken the alignment between systems if place identity is not maintained. That is why reconciled deliverables begin to age as soon as they are produced. A layer, geodatabase, spreadsheet, report, or export may be accurate at the time of delivery, but it is still a snapshot. Future work often repeats portions of the same matching and review process because the reconciliation result was not maintained as a durable registry.

Masora is designed to make those snapshots outputs of a maintained registry rather than the final state of the work.

The missing primitive: stable place identity

The missing primitive is not a map, address, parcel, or GIS feature. It is stable place identity.

In Masora, that identity is represented by a Location Record: a durable record for a real-world place. While addresses, geometries, and source records may change over time, the Location Record remains stable. That allows systems to reference the same place without depending on a particular file, source record, address spelling, or geometry. The result is a shift from one-time reconciliation to a maintained identity layer for places.

A worked example

Consider a single house. In the assessment system it is a situs address tied to a parcel ID, spelled “123 Main St.” In the utility system it is a service location tied to a billing account, recorded as “123 Main Street” with a slightly different coordinate. In the GIS address-point layer it is a point feature with its own object ID, placed at the structure's rooftop. In permitting it appears as “123 N. Main St” on an open building permit. A public-safety dataset carries an access point near the driveway.

Five systems, five identifiers, five slightly different representations — each correct for its own purpose, none aware of the others. A Masora Location Record sits beneath them:

Location Record masora:loc:7f3a…c1 stable, permanent │ ├─ assessment 123 Main St parcel 17-024-018 ├─ utility 123 Main Street acct 4471902 ├─ gis address pt (rooftop point) OBJECTID 88213 ├─ permitting 123 N. Main St PERMIT-2026-0512 └─ public safety driveway access pt ESN 043 ! conflict utility vs gis coordinate — surfaced for review

One durable identifier; five source records linked to it; the disagreement kept, not erased.

When the utility coordinate and the GIS point disagree about where the structure sits, that disagreement is recorded as a conflict against the Location Record and surfaced for review rather than silently overwritten. If the parcel later splits, or the address is renamed to “123 Main Avenue,” the Location Record persists, the source links are updated, and the change is preserved as history.

What Masora maintains

Masora takes source records from the systems a jurisdiction already runs and links the records that refer to the same real-world place. For each place, Masora maintains a Location Record with stable identity, linked source records, current address and geometry, evidence, conflicts, review history, external references, status, and publication state.

The registry is designed to be inspectable and auditable. Conflicts are treated as first-class records rather than hidden warnings or quality scores. When sources disagree about a material fact, Masora records the disagreement, links it to the affected Location Record, and supports review and resolution.

Human judgment remains part of the process. Routine matches can be handled automatically, while ambiguous cases are surfaced for review. Decisions become part of the registry's history, creating an auditable trail of how place identity changed over time.

Where Masora fits

Masora is intended to complement existing systems, not replace them. GIS platforms, dispatch systems, assessment systems, permitting systems, utility systems, public works systems, spatial databases, and custom applications continue serving their operational roles. Masora provides a separate layer beneath them: a maintained record of which source records refer to the same real-world place.

Readers familiar with enterprise data management will recognize this as entity resolution applied to places: deciding when separate records describe the same real-world thing and maintaining an authoritative “golden record” for it. Master data management tools apply that idea to customers, products, or vendors. Masora applies it to physical places, with the geometry handling, spatial reasoning, and GIS publication that local-government place data requires and that general-purpose MDM tools do not provide.

Masora is designed to integrate with existing GIS environments. In ArcGIS-based environments, the maintained registry can be published through familiar geospatial services, exports, and synchronization workflows. In open-source or mixed environments, it can support spatial databases, QGIS workflows, file-based exports, APIs, and other local-government data pipelines. The integration model does not depend on a single GIS brand. The underlying need is the same across environments: maps, layers, applications, and operational systems need a stable way to refer to the same real-world places over time. This is especially relevant where GIS data supports public safety, NG9‑1‑1 readiness, asset management, utilities, and other workflows that depend on current, trusted location references.

For partner firms, Masora creates a durable delivery model around work they already perform. Firms can help clients identify source systems, validate matches, review exceptions, publish the registry into downstream applications, and establish long-term governance. Masora maintains the place registry; the firm provides the domain expertise, implementation support, and stewardship needed to make it useful in the client's operating environment.

Security and control

Masora is designed for public-sector security and governance requirements. Customers retain ownership of their data, systems, and access controls. The platform follows least-privilege principles, integrates with existing identity systems, and is designed to operate without requiring broad administrative access to customer environments. This matters because a maintained place registry must be straightforward to review, scope, approve, and revoke within existing government security processes.

What we want to learn

Masora is seeking conversations with firms and jurisdictions that regularly encounter address, GIS, parcel, asset, utility, public-safety, and operational data reconciliation problems. The practical questions are where reconciliation work breaks down today, which source systems should anchor an initial registry, how matches and conflicts should be reviewed, and what security, governance, and GIS integration requirements would need to be met.

We are ready to stand up an initial registry with an early jurisdiction or delivery partner. The goal is to determine whether stable place identity should become a maintained layer beneath local-government GIS and operational systems.
